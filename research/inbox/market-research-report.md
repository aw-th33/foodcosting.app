## 1. Market Size & Opportunity

### Total Addressable Market (TAM)

- **US restaurant industry:** ~1 million independent restaurants, plus ~300K food trucks, caterers, and home-based food businesses
- **Food costing software TAM (US):** Estimated $300–500M annually
- **Underserved segment:** 60–70% of independent food businesses use no dedicated food costing tool — they rely on spreadsheets, pen-and-paper, or nothing at all
- **Market growth:** 12–15% CAGR driven by margin pressure, food inflation, and digital adoption among SMBs

### Serviceable Addressable Market (SAM)

- **Target segment:** Independent restaurants, food trucks, caterers, home bakers — businesses with <$2M annual revenue that cannot justify $100+/month software
- **Estimated SAM:** ~500,000–700,000 businesses in the US alone
- **At $15/month:** Theoretical revenue ceiling of $63–79M ARR if fully penetrated (unrealistic but directionally useful)

### Serviceable Obtainable Market (SOM) — Realistic 3-Year Target

- **Conservative:** 1,000–3,000 paying customers → $180K–$540K ARR
- **Moderate:** 3,000–5,000 paying customers → $540K–$900K ARR
- **Aggressive:** 5,000–10,000 paying customers → $900K–$1.8M ARR

### Why Now

- Food inflation has made cost management critical for survival, not just optimization
- Younger food business owners expect digital tools but reject enterprise complexity
- No dominant player owns the <$20/month price tier for food costing

**Risk:** The gap exists partly because the economics of serving $15/month customers are challenging. More on this in the risk section.

## 3. Revenue Potential & Unit Economics

### Customer Lifetime Value (LTV)

| Scenario | Monthly Churn | Avg Lifetime | LTV (Monthly) | LTV (Annual Plan) |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| **Optimistic** | 3% | 33 months | $495 | $660+ |
| **Base case** | 5% | 20 months | $300 | $500+ |
| **Pessimistic** | 8% | 12.5 months | $188 | $300+ |

- **Annual plans ($149/year) roughly double effective LTV** by reducing churn and locking in revenue
- **Lifetime deal ($99/year)** is a one-time acquisition tool — good for early traction, bad for long-term economics

### Customer Acquisition Cost (CAC) Constraints

- **Target CAC:** Must stay below $100 (ideally <$50) at $15/month pricing
- **Paid acquisition (Google Ads, Facebook):** Likely $30–80 CAC for food business SaaS — viable but tight at $15/month
- **Organic acquisition (SEO, community):** Near-zero marginal CAC — this is the primary viable channel
- **LTV:CAC ratio target:** 3:1 minimum → at $300 LTV, max sustainable CAC is $100

### Revenue Milestones

| Milestone | Customers | MRR | ARR |
|---|---|---|---|
| Ramen profitable | 200 | $3,000 | $36,000 |
| Meaningful side income | 667 | $10,000 | $120,000 |
| Full-time viable | 2,000 | $30,000 | $360,000 |
| Growth stage | 5,000 | $75,000 | $900,000 |

### Gross Margins

- **Software delivery costs:** Hosting, infrastructure, Lovable platform → estimated 10–15% of revenue
- **Gross margin:** 85–90% (typical for SaaS)
- **Net margin (solo founder):** 70–80% at scale (before marketing spend)

## 5. Risk Assessment

### High Risk

| Risk | Impact | Mitigation |
|---|---|---|
| **Low price = unsustainable CAC** | Can't afford paid acquisition channels | Double down on organic (SEO, community). Consider price increase to $19–25/month once value is proven |
| **Churn at SMB tier** | Small food businesses fail at high rates (60% close within 3 years) | Annual plans, sticky workflows, low-effort onboarding |
| **Solo founder bandwidth** | Constrained hours limit growth velocity | Agent automation, ruthless prioritization, resist feature creep |

### Medium Risk

| Risk | Impact | Mitigation |
|---|---|---|
| **Enterprise player goes downmarket** | MarketMan or Toast builds a free/cheap tier | Speed advantage — they're slow to simplify. Simplicity is hard to retrofit |
| **CostBrain or new entrant competes directly** | Price war at the low end | Brand and community trust are the moat, not features |
| **Lovable platform dependency** | Locked into a third-party builder | Acceptable for now. Revisit at $30K+ MRR if migration becomes necessary |

### Low Risk

| Risk | Impact | Mitigation |
|---|---|---|
| **Market shrinks** | Fewer food businesses | Food industry is resilient — people always eat. Counter-cyclical in some segments |
| **AI disrupts food costing** | ChatGPT does recipe costing for free | Free tools lack persistence, saved recipes, ingredient databases. App > prompt |

## 6. Go / No-Go Assessment

### The Case FOR (Go)

1. **Clear market gap.** No credible player owns the <$20/month food costing niche. The demand exists — 60%+ of independents have no tool.
2. **Extremely low breakeven.** 7–14 customers cover costs. This is a business that can survive and compound with very little capital.
3. **High gross margins.** 85–90% margins mean every customer dollar mostly flows to the bottom line.
4. **Organic distribution is working.** SEO keywords are trending from 40+ to 20+ positions. Community engagement has produced real signups. These are compounding channels.
5. **Automation leverage.** Agent workflows mean a solo founder can operate with the output of a small team.
6. **Low downside.** Monthly costs are <$200. If it doesn't work, the financial loss is minimal.

### The Case AGAINST (No-Go / Caution)

1. **$15/month pricing may cap growth.** Paid acquisition is barely viable. Scaling past organic channels will require a price increase or add-on revenue.
2. **SMB churn is structural.** Small food businesses churn for reasons unrelated to your product (they close). This limits LTV.
3. **Solo founder risk.** Everything depends on Ahmed's bandwidth and motivation. No redundancy.

### Verdict: **GO — with conditions**

The opportunity is real, the economics work at small scale, and the risk is low. This is a viable side-project-to-real-business path.

**Conditions for continued investment:**

1. **Hit 100 paying customers within 6 months** as a validation milestone. If organic channels can't produce this, the distribution thesis needs revisiting.
2. **Plan a price increase to $19–25/month** once 200+ customers validate willingness to pay. This dramatically improves unit economics and makes paid acquisition viable.
3. **Push annual plans aggressively.** They double LTV and reduce churn. Consider making annual the default with monthly as the alternative.
4. **Do not add features for growth.** The product is sufficient. Distribution is the bottleneck. Every hour spent on product instead of distribution delays validation.
5. **Track CAC and churn from day one.** These two numbers determine whether the business scales or plateaus.
