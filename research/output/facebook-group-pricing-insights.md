# foodcosting.app - Facebook Group Pricing Insights

**Date:** April 23, 2026
**Status:** Working research note
**Scope:** Community demand, competitor framing, and positioning

---

## Executive Summary

Recent discussions in the "Restaurant Owners and Managers" Facebook group point to a clear and repeated pain point: restaurant operators struggle to price items with confidence.

The problem is not limited to calculating food cost. Operators are trying to connect:

- ingredient cost
- food cost percentage
- gross profit per item
- market willingness to pay
- card processing fees
- packaging and operating costs

This suggests that the real job-to-be-done is not "calculate my food cost." It is "help me decide what to charge and understand whether an item is worth selling."

---

## Key Demand Signals

### 1. Pricing confusion is frequent and emotionally important

The most engaging posts are about practical pricing decisions:

- should pricing be based on food cost percentage or dollar profit?
- how should coffee be priced?
- how should card fees be handled?
- when is a higher food cost percentage acceptable?

This is a strong signal that pricing is not a solved problem for many operators.

### 2. Food cost percentage is the entry point, not the final answer

The strongest responses in the group do not rely on rigid rules like `3x`, `4x`, or `5x`.

Instead, better operators talk about:

- contribution margin
- market price ceilings
- menu mix
- loss leaders and perception anchors
- whole-menu profitability

This means users want clarity and confidence, not just formulas.

### 3. Operators want structure, but not complexity

Many people are asking for spreadsheets, sharing screenshots of costing sheets, or requesting templates.

That tells us:

- the need is real
- people want a repeatable system
- they do not necessarily want a large or expensive software suite

---

## Competitor Landscape

### Competitor Type 1: AI-looking "vibecoded" tools

Observed pattern:

- broad positioning
- too many features
- overwhelming interfaces
- low-trust feel

Takeaway:

These tools may validate the demand, but they also create an opportunity for a product that feels more focused, practical, and trustworthy.

### Competitor Type 2: Restaurant inventory/operations systems

Observed pattern:

- priced around `$80-$160/month`
- designed for broader operations management
- heavier workflows
- often solve larger back-office problems

Takeaway:

These systems are likely too expensive and too broad for operators who simply want fast pricing and profitability answers.

### Real Default Competitor: Excel / Google Sheets

This appears to be the most important competitive insight.

People are already solving the problem with:

- spreadsheets
- copied templates
- manual formulas
- calculator-based workflows

Why this matters:

- spreadsheets are cheap
- spreadsheets feel flexible and familiar
- spreadsheets make the math visible
- spreadsheets are easy to share in operator communities

This means foodcosting.app is not only competing against software products. It is competing against the current spreadsheet workflow that users already trust enough to use.

---

## Strategic Product Insight

The market does not need another bloated "restaurant software" product.

There is a gap between:

- messy but flexible spreadsheets
- expensive all-in-one restaurant systems
- noisy AI-first tools that try to do too much

That gap is likely where foodcosting.app should live.

Possible category framing:

> The simplest dedicated pricing and profitability tool between spreadsheets and expensive restaurant systems.

---

## Positioning Implications

foodcosting.app should likely be framed less as a generic costing calculator and more as a focused pricing decision tool.

Promising directions:

- help operators know what to charge
- show food cost %, fees, and profit per item in one place
- make the math transparent
- stay simple and fast for one-item or menu-level decisions
- avoid becoming a bloated all-in-one operations suite

Potential positioning language:

- "Stop guessing what to charge."
- "Price every item with confidence."
- "See food cost, fees, and profit per item in one place."
- "Everything you do in a costing spreadsheet, without broken formulas."

---

## What We Should Learn From Spreadsheet Behavior

If people constantly ask for sheets, they are asking for structure.

But they are also signaling what any replacement must preserve:

- flexibility
- transparency
- affordability
- ease of use

To beat spreadsheets, foodcosting.app likely needs to be:

- faster than a sheet
- easier to understand than a sheet
- harder to break than a sheet
- clearly built for restaurant pricing workflows

---

## Practical Product Direction

Features and messaging should stay close to the observed pain:

- item-level costing
- pricing suggestions or price testing
- card fee handling
- packaging/consumables support
- gross profit per item
- visibility into percentage vs dollar margin
- simple menu-level context without becoming a full inventory suite

---

## Bottom Line

The Facebook group signal is strong:

- pricing confusion is common
- the pain is repeated across food, drinks, and fees
- existing tools are either too messy, too broad, or too expensive
- spreadsheets are the true default competitor

The opportunity for foodcosting.app is to become the focused, trustworthy, spreadsheet-replacing tool for restaurant pricing and profitability decisions.
