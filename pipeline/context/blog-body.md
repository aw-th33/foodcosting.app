Most food business owners know food cost matters. Fewer know what their specific number should actually be. "Keep it low" is not a target. This post gives you the real benchmarks by business type, explains why the numbers differ, and tells you the single most common mistake that blows each one up.

## What "ideal food cost" actually means

Food cost percentage is the share of your menu price that goes toward ingredients. If a dish costs $4 to make and sells for $14, your food cost is 28.5%. The word "ideal" just means the range where your food cost leaves enough room to cover labour, rent, and profit without running the business on thin air. It is not a universal number. It shifts based on your format, your volume, and what else is eating your margin.

<callout icon="💡">
**The benchmark that matters:** Food cost percentage is the fastest signal you have that pricing is working or isn't. Get yours below your target and keep it there.
</callout>

## Ideal food cost percentage for restaurants

**Target range: 28–35%**

Full-service restaurants typically land between 28% and 35%, with most operators aiming for 30–32% as their working target. Quick-service and fast casual tend to sit a little higher (32–35%) because smaller average ticket sizes mean you need food cost to absorb more of each sale.

The biggest driver is protein. Beef, seafood, and poultry are expensive, and if they anchor your menu, you will feel cost pressure that a pasta-heavy menu does not have. A seafood restaurant running 34% is probably doing fine. A burger spot at the same number needs to look harder at its portion weights.

The most common mistake: pricing new dishes based on memory rather than an actual recipe cost. Costs shift. A ribeye that worked at $22 in January may not work at $22 in April.

## Ideal food cost percentage for food trucks

**Target range: 28–32%**

Food trucks run at a tighter range than brick-and-mortar restaurants because of how the cost structure works. Your overhead is lower: no lease, smaller crew, no dining room to maintain. That lower overhead means your margins have to come mostly from the food itself. You cannot lean on a $10,000-a-month lease to justify premium pricing the way a sit-down restaurant can.

Running above 32% on a truck is a real problem because there is not much else to cut. You cannot reduce rent you do not have. The labour pool is small. If food cost is bleeding, it is usually portion creep (the scoops getting a little heavier each week) or raw material prices rising without a corresponding menu price increase.

Watch for this: truck operators tend to hold prices steady because raising them feels risky with a captive lunch crowd. Meanwhile, margins quietly collapse.

## Ideal food cost percentage for caterers

**Target range: 25–35%**

Catering has a wider range than the other segments because you know your headcount before you cook. You buy what you need (or close to it). Waste should be minimal. A well-run catering operation can hit 25–28% consistently, which is the best food cost benchmark in the food industry.

The upper end of the range (30–35%) shows up when menu complexity is high or when contracts involve last-minute headcount changes. Corporate catering and wedding catering are not the same business: one is predictable and volume-driven, the other involves buffer prep that costs you regardless of who actually shows up.

<callout icon="⚠️" color="red_bg">
**Watch out:** Never quote a catering job without costing the full recipe, including oil, garnish, serving supplies, and packaging. Those small lines add 3–5 percentage points if you ignore them.
</callout>

The most common mistake: underestimating buffer prep. Catering 80 people often means cooking for 90. That extra 12.5% goes into your food cost whether the guests eat it or not.

## Ideal food cost percentage for home bakers

**Target range: 30–40%**

Home bakers and cottage bakery operators run at a higher food cost than any other segment, and that is not a failure of business skill. It is a function of scale.

Baking is ingredient-intensive. Sugar, butter, eggs, flour, chocolate, and custom decorating supplies are not cheap when you are buying retail. You are not getting the bulk pricing a restaurant chain gets. A custom cake that costs $28 in ingredients and sells for $80 is at 35% food cost, which is healthy for this format. That same cake priced at $60 puts you at 47%, which is unsustainable.

Yield loss hits harder at small scale too. A batch of macarons where 20% crack and cannot be sold is a cost with no corresponding revenue. Waste is part of the calculation, not an accident.

The biggest thing people miss: packaging. Boxes, ribbon, tissue paper, sticker labels, and delivery containers are part of your product cost. Leaving them out makes your food cost look better than it is, until you check your bank account.

## What to do if you are over your target

Start with portion sizes, not pricing. Most operators instinctively want to raise menu prices when food cost is high. Sometimes that is right, but before you go there, check whether your portions have crept up. A quarter-ounce more chicken per plate across 80 covers a day is a real cost that a price increase does not necessarily fix.

After portions, look at your supplier costs. Have your primary ingredient prices changed in the last six months without you adjusting? Most vendors do not announce price increases loudly. You find out when your invoices quietly go up and your food cost follows.

Then revisit pricing. If your portions are correct and your supplier costs are market rate, a price increase is the right answer. Run the numbers: a $1 increase on a dish that sells 40 times a day is $1,200 a month in additional gross margin.

<details>
<summary>A quick three-step check when your food cost is running high</summary>

1. Pull your last 30 days of ingredient invoices and compare to the same period last year. Identify which lines moved most.
2. Re-weigh three of your highest-volume dishes against the written recipe. Portion drift is more common than most owners expect.
3. Recalculate food cost on your five best-selling items at current ingredient prices. If any are above your target, reprice them.

</details>

## How to calculate yours right now

The formula is simple: divide the total ingredient cost of a dish by its menu price, then multiply by 100.

A burger that costs $3.80 to make and sells for $12.00 is running at **31.7% food cost** (3.80 / 12.00 x 100). That sits inside the restaurant target range. Price that same burger at $10.00 and you are at 38%, above target, and losing margin every time the grill fires.

The harder part is not the formula. It is having accurate, current ingredient costs for every item on your menu. Most operators are working from costs they last checked six months ago, which means their food cost calculations are wrong before they even start.
